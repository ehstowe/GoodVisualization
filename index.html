What is a good visualization?

First, here are two examples of bad visualizations.

On December 2, 2018, AL.com published an article titled "Alabama public school enrollment down, Hispanic student population growing." The text of the article is overwhelmingly full of statistics. The article is a perfect occasion to display data visually... and the attempt to take advantage of this opportunity was feeble at best. The article contains these two tables which are as hard to understand as the statistic-filled article itself.

Read the entire article here.

This information could be better presented in a different way. For example, a slope chart could better indicate the speed at which different schools' populations changed over time.



Now, here is an example of a great visualization.

On January 26, 2019, The New York Times published an article titled "How Every Member Got to Congress," that included a powerful visualization tracking the "major educational, career, and political milestones" of the members of the House of Representatives. Read the article here.

The visualization has a similar format to the visualization of the emergence of neuroscience highlighted in Alberto Cairo's The Truthful Art. It seeks to enlighten and inform readers, not to entertain or sell a product. It is easy to understand because of the contrast of red and blue, the sections of thicker and thinner lines, and the interactive feature that highlights each representative's track. The visualization changes when it is shrunk to a smaller screen - it transforms into several smaller, more specific segments of the original. This prevents the screen from becoming too cluttered. Ultimately, the visualization meets Cairo's five qualities of great visualizations - it is truthful, functional, beautiful, insightful, and enlightening.
